\section{Literature Review}

Skin lesion segmentation is a fundamental preprocessing step in
computer-aided diagnosis (CAD) systems for dermatological conditions.
Clinically, dermatologists rely on the ABCDE criteria (asymmetry,
border irregularity, color variation, diameter, and evolution) to
assess malignancy risk~\cite{Nachbar1994ABCD} (Figure~\ref{fig:abcde}).
Each of these criteria is inherently spatial: asymmetry requires
comparing lesion halves, border irregularity depends on precise
boundary delineation, and color variation analysis is meaningful
only within a correctly isolated lesion region.
Consequently, the accuracy of the segmentation mask directly
conditions the reliability of downstream diagnostic features.
This tight coupling between \emph{segmentation quality and
diagnostic outcome} has driven sustained effort toward building
more accurate and robust lesion segmentation models, with deep
learning architectures at the center of this progress.

Early deep learning approaches to lesion segmentation were dominated by fully convolutional encoder-decoder architectures.
The seminal U-Net~\cite{Ronneberger2015UNet}, originally proposed for biomedical image segmentation,
became the de facto backbone for skin lesion tasks due to its skip connections that preserve spatial detail across encoding and decoding stages.
Building on this foundation, the ISIC challenges~\cite{Codella2017ISIC, Codella2019ISIC2018}
provided the first large-scale benchmarks for skin lesion segmentation,
establishing U-Net and its variants as the dominant approach and catalysing a wave of architecture-level improvements.

Attention mechanisms were incorporated to allow the decoder to focus selectively on lesion boundaries rather than treating all spatial locations equally~\cite{Oktay2018AttentionUNet}.
Dilated convolutions and atrous spatial pyramid pooling, popularised by DeepLabV3+~\cite{Chen2018DeepLab}, improved multi-scale context aggregation and were quickly adapted to dermoscopy segmentation to handle lesions that vary greatly in size and aspect ratio.
The introduction of \emph{transformer-based architectures} marked a further shift: TransUNet~\cite{Chen2021TransUNet} combined a Vision Transformer encoder with a convolutional decoder, capturing long-range spatial dependencies that convolutions struggle to model, while Swin-UNet~\cite{Cao2021SwinUNet} demonstrated that a \emph{pure transformer hierarchy} could match or exceed CNN-based segmentation performance on medical benchmarks.

Despite these architectural advances, a \emph{persistent bottleneck remains}: all of the above models are supervised and rely on pixel-level annotated masks for training.
Data scarcity is a well-documented challenge in deep learning broadly~\cite{bansal2022systematic}, and dermoscopy is particularly affected: the limited scale of annotated datasets such as ISIC has been shown to hinder the development of highly generalisable segmentation models~\cite{cassidy2022analysis}.
Public dermoscopy collections provide only on the order of ten thousand annotated images, and expert annotation is costly and slow to scale.
Consequently, models trained on existing datasets frequently \emph{fail to generalise} to unseen lesion morphologies or acquisition conditions a ceiling imposed not by architecture but by data scarcity.
This observation motivates the exploration of synthetic data generation as a complementary strategy to architectural refinement.

Synthetic image generation has therefore emerged as a principled
response to this bottleneck, offering a scalable path toward
more generalizable segmentation models without the cost and
delay of expert annotation.Early approaches to synthetic dermoscopy image generation relied on
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)~\cite{goodfellow2014generative}.
In the general medical imaging domain, architectures such as DCGAN and
StyleGAN2 were applied to augment under-represented pathology classes,
with StyleGAN2 achieving near-indistinguishable synthetic outputs on
radiology datasets as measured by Fréchet Inception Distance
(FID)~\cite{ktena2024generative}.
For skin lesion data specifically, conditional GAN variants were
adapted to address the severe class imbalance in dermoscopic
collections, where malignant samples constitute a small minority of
available images~\cite{cassidy2022analysis}.

However, noise-based GAN architectures generate images without
explicit spatial constraints, producing lesions whose morphological
markers lack anatomical coherence.
This limitation is critical for segmentation: training a segmentation
model requires pixel-perfect ground-truth masks that are spatially
aligned with the synthesised image.
Bissoto~et~al.~\cite{bissoto2018skin} addressed this by adopting
pix2pixHD~\cite{wang2018pix2pixHD}, a high-resolution
image-to-image translation network conditioned on semantic
segmentation masks.
By incorporating superpixel-based instance maps alongside binary
lesion masks, their framework produced 1024×512 synthetic dermoscopy
images whose structural boundaries remained faithful to the input
mask, achieving an AUC of 82.0\% on a downstream classification task.
A subsequent critical review by the same group confirmed that
mask-conditioned translation models such as pix2pixHD and
Spatially Adaptive Normalisation (SPADE) consistently outperform
noise-based generators in anatomical
coherence~\cite{bissoto2021gan}.

Despite these advances, GAN-based mask-conditioned synthesis
suffers from training instability, mode collapse, and limited
diversity in generated textures.
Diffusion models have recently emerged as a more stable and
expressive alternative: by learning to reverse a gradual noising
process, they produce sharper and more diverse outputs than GANs
while remaining amenable to spatial conditioning
signals~\cite{ho2020denoising} (Figure~\ref{fig:gan_vs_diffusion}).
This property makes them particularly suited to the two-stage
pipeline explored in the present work, in which a diffusion model
first synthesises a plausible lesion mask and subsequently generates
a photorealistic dermoscopy image conditioned on that mask ---
decoupling shape generation from texture synthesis and enabling
fine-grained control over both.

This stability advantage has been validated in the dermoscopy domain.
Yang~et~al.~\cite{yang2025better} demonstrated that a DDPM augmented
with dilated convolutions and self-attention mechanisms could reduce
the annotated data requirement substantially: lightweight segmentation
models trained on real and synthetic combined data matched the
performance of models with ten times more parameters trained on real
data alone.
At the architecture level, SkinDualGen~\cite{xu2025skindualgen}
extended Stable Diffusion with LoRA fine-tuning to simultaneously
synthesise a dermoscopic image and its corresponding binary
segmentation mask from a single text prompt, achieving a Dice score
of 80.12 and an IoU of 66.84 on ISIC 2018 --- removing the need for
separate mask annotation entirely.
ControlNet-based adaptations of mask-conditioned diffusion further
demonstrated that providing an explicit lesion boundary as a spatial
conditioning signal enables precise control over lesion shape and
location in the generated image~\cite{du2024boosting}.

Taken together, this body of evidence positions mask-guided diffusion
as the most promising generative strategy for dermoscopy segmentation:
it combines the anatomical controllability of mask-conditioned GANs
with the training stability and output diversity of diffusion models.
The present work builds directly on this foundation.
