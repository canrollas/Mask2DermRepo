\paragraph{Dataset.}
We use HAM10000~\cite{Tschandl2018HAM10000} as our training source. Among publicly available dermoscopy benchmarks, HAM10000 offers the largest collection of expert-annotated pixel-level segmentation masks, comprising 10,015 images acquired across multiple institutions and imaging devices. This scale and acquisition diversity make it well-suited for training a generative model that must capture a broad range of lesion morphologies and imaging conditions. The dataset is split into training (80\%), validation (10\%), and held-out test (10\%) sets.

\paragraph{Optics-inspired standardization.}
Raw images sourced from heterogeneous devices differ in field-of-view shape, brightness roll-off, and lens geometry. To harmonize these differences, we implement a physics-based simulation of dermoscope optics consisting of three stages applied in sequence.

First, we define the radial distance $d(x, y) = \sqrt{(x - c_x)^2 + (y - c_y)^2}$ from the image centre $(c_x, c_y)$. This distance map drives both the circular aperture mask $M(d)$ and the vignetting falloff $V(d)$:
\begin{equation}
    M(d) = \begin{cases}
               1, & d \leq R \\
               \text{smooth feather}, & R < d \leq R + \delta \\
               0, & d > R + \delta
    \end{cases}
    \quad \text{and} \quad
    V(d) = (1 - \alpha) + \alpha \left( 1 - (d/R)^2 \right)^\gamma
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the aperture radius, $\delta$ is the feathering margin, $\alpha$ controls vignetting strength, and $\gamma$ governs the falloff rate.

Second, we simulate barrel distortion by remapping pixel coordinates via a radial displacement model:
\begin{align}
    X &= \frac{x - c_x}{f_x}, \quad Y = \frac{y - c_y}{f_y}, \quad r^2 = X^2 + Y^2 \nonumber \\
    x_d &= f_x X(1 + k_1 r^2) + c_x, \quad y_d = f_y Y(1 + k_1 r^2) + c_y
\end{align}
where $k_1 < 0$ induces the barrel effect. The final standardized image combines all three components: $I'(x, y) = I(x_d, y_d) \cdot M(d) \cdot V(d)$.

\begin{figure}[H]

    \caption{Physics-based optical simulation pipeline applied to raw dermoscopy images. \textbf{Stage 1} computes the radial distance map $d(x,y)$ and derives the aperture mask $M(d)$ and vignetting map $V(d)$. \textbf{Stage 2} remaps pixel coordinates via barrel distortion to produce $I(x_d, y_d)$. \textbf{Stage 3} multiplies the distorted image by $M(d)\!\cdot\!V(d)$ to yield the standardized image $I'(x,y)$.}

\end{figure}

\paragraph{Contrast enhancement.}
Dermoscopic images acquired under varying illumination conditions often exhibit low local contrast, obscuring fine structural details such as pigment networks and vascular patterns. To address this, we apply Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE)~\cite{pizer1987adaptive} to the luminance channel of each image in the LAB colour space. Unlike global histogram equalization, CLAHE operates on local image tiles of size $8\times8$ pixels, independently redistributing intensity values within each tile. A clip limit $\lambda$ caps the amplification of any histogram bin before redistribution, preventing noise over-amplification in near-uniform regions:
\begin{equation}
    H'(v) = \min\!\left(H(v),\, \lambda \cdot \frac{N_{\text{tile}}}{L}\right)
\end{equation}
where $H(v)$ is the histogram count at intensity $v$, $N_{\text{tile}}$ is the number of pixels per tile, and $L$ is the number of intensity levels. The clipped excess is redistributed uniformly across all bins. We use a clip limit of $\lambda=2.0$ and bilinear interpolation at tile boundaries to avoid blocking artefacts.

\paragraph{Data augmentation.}
To improve generalization and reduce overfitting, we apply stochastic augmentation exclusively to the training split. Geometric transforms (random horizontal and vertical flips with $p=0.5$, random rotation up to $30^\circ$) are applied identically to both the image and its mask to preserve spatial alignment. Colour augmentation is applied to the image only: brightness, contrast, saturation, and hue are perturbed with factors of $0.3$, $0.3$, $0.4$, and $0.08$ respectively.

\paragraph{Normalization and mask conditioning.}
After augmentation, images are normalized to $[-1, 1]$ via $\hat{I} = (I - 0.5)/0.5$. Binary segmentation masks undergo separate conditioning preparation: each mask is resized to $512\times512$ using nearest-neighbour interpolation to preserve sharp boundaries, binarized at threshold 127, and replicated across three channels to form an RGB tensor in $[0, 1]$.

\subsection{Model Architecture}\label{sec:arch}

Mask2Derm maps a binary lesion mask to a photorealistic dermoscopic image conditioned on a free-form text prompt. Achieving this requires a generative model that simultaneously produces clinically realistic skin texture, respects the spatial extent of the lesion specified by the mask, and responds to free-form text prompts describing the desired image appearance. Training such a model from scratch on a small medical dataset is infeasible: the training corpus does not provide sufficient diversity for the model to generalize across imaging devices, lesion morphologies, and acquisition conditions. Mask2Derm therefore adopts a transfer-learning strategy, building on RealVisXL V4.0~\cite{SG161222RealVisXL}, a photorealism-focused fine-tune of Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL)~\cite{podell2023sdxl} built on the latent diffusion framework~\cite{Rombach2022LDM}. RealVisXL contributes learned representations of skin appearance, lighting, and photographic realism; the domain adaptation task reduces to conditioning this pretrained model on dermoscopy-specific mask and text inputs while preserving its existing visual knowledge.

Before describing the full training loop, it is helpful to understand the four fundamental operations the pipeline performs at every step: \emph{(i)}~compressing the input image into a compact latent representation, \emph{(ii)}~corrupting that representation with structured noise, \emph{(iii)}~encoding the text prompt into a vector the model can attend to, and \emph{(iv)}~conditioning generation spatially via the binary lesion mask. Each is explained below.

\paragraph{Step 1: Compressing the image into a latent representation.}
The term \emph{latent} (from the Latin \emph{latere}, meaning ``to lie hidden'') refers to variables that are not directly observed but are inferred to govern the structure of observable data. In our case, the latent space is a compressed, abstract representation of an image that retains its essential visual content but discards redundant pixel-level detail.

Performing diffusion directly in pixel space at $512\times512$ is computationally prohibitive: each denoising step would operate on $512\times512\times3\approx786{,}000$ values. Instead, Mask2Derm first projects every image into a far more compact space using a Variational Autoencoder (VAE)~\cite{Rombach2022LDM}. A VAE is a generative model that learns to encode high-dimensional inputs into a low-dimensional latent distribution, and to reconstruct the original from samples of that distribution. The frozen VAE encoder $\mathcal{E}$ maps each $512\times512$ RGB image $\mathbf{x}$ to a $64\times64\times4$ latent tensor:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_0 = \mathcal{E}(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^{64\times64\times4}
\end{equation}
This is an $8\times$ spatial reduction ($64^2 / 512^2 = 1/64$ of the original element count), yet the compression is nearly lossless for natural image content: the paired VAE decoder $\mathcal{D}$ recovers perceptually sharp images from the latent. All subsequent noise addition, denoising, and loss computation occur in this compact $64\times64\times4$ space. The VAE weights are never updated during training. Figure~\ref{fig:vae} illustrates the compression process on a representative skin lesion image, showing the four individual latent channels that encode the compressed image content.

\paragraph{Step 2: Corrupting the latent with structured noise.}
Once the clean latent $\mathbf{z}_0$ is obtained, the DDPM forward process gradually corrupts it by adding Gaussian noise over $T=1000$ discrete timesteps. At any timestep $t$, the noisy latent is given by:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{z}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\alpha}_t$ is a monotonically decreasing sequence controlled by the noise schedule. At $t=0$ the latent is nearly clean; at $t=T$ it is indistinguishable from pure Gaussian noise. The model is trained to \emph{reverse} this process: given $\mathbf{z}_t$ and the timestep $t$, it predicts the noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ that was added, and thereby recovers $\mathbf{z}_0$. Figure~\ref{fig:noise} shows how a real skin lesion latent evolves as noise is progressively applied across timesteps.

Starting from the clean latent $\mathbf{z}_0$, Gaussian noise is added according to $\mathbf{z}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{z}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$. By $t=999$ the original structure is completely obscured. The denoising U-Net learns to reverse this process, recovering $\mathbf{z}_0$ step by step.}

\paragraph{Step 3: Encoding the text prompt into a conditioning vector.}
Text prompts are discrete token sequences; the U-Net operates on continuous tensors. To bridge this gap, SDXL passes each prompt through two CLIP text encoders in parallel~\cite{podell2023sdxl}. The first encoder (\texttt{CLIPTextModel}) produces token-level semantic features of shape $[B,\,77,\,768]$ from its penultimate transformer layer. The second encoder (\texttt{CLIPTextModelWithProjection}) contributes complementary high-dimensional features of shape $[B,\,77,\,1280]$ together with a pooled global embedding of shape $[B,\,1280]$. The two token sequences are concatenated along the feature dimension to form the joint conditioning embedding $\mathbf{c}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{B\times77\times2048}$. Inside the U-Net, every transformer block attends to $\mathbf{c}_t$ through cross-attention, allowing the text prompt to modulate generated content at every spatial scale, from global appearance down to fine colour and texture detail. The pooled global embedding is additionally fused with size-conditioning metadata and projected into the timestep embedding, giving the model a coarse scene-level summary alongside the per-token signal. Both CLIP encoders remain frozen throughout training. Figure~\ref{fig:clip} illustrates this dual-encoder encoding pipeline.

\paragraph{Step 4: Spatial conditioning via ControlNet.}
The three components above (latent representation, noisy latent, and text embedding) tell the model \emph{what} to generate, but not \emph{where} the lesion should appear. This spatial constraint is provided by a binary segmentation mask $\mathbf{c}_s \in \{0,1\}^{512\times512}$, which is replicated across three channels to form an RGB-format $[0,1]$ tensor and fed into the ControlNet.

The ControlNet is a trainable copy of the frozen U-Net encoder, initialized from its weights so that it starts with the same visual priors. Because it is initialized this way (rather than from a pretrained segmentation model), it carries no domain-specific segmentation bias; it learns from scratch to translate mask geometry into generation guidance. The mask passes through the ControlNet's encoder and bottleneck at multiple spatial resolutions, producing a set of \emph{residual} feature maps at each level. These residuals are injected into the corresponding skip connections of the frozen U-Net decoder via zero-initialized $1\times1$ convolutions (zero convolutions). At initialization the zero convolutions output exactly zero, so the frozen backbone is completely unaffected; as training progresses they gradually open up, and the ControlNet learns to steer where the lesion appears without disrupting the U-Net's pretrained texture knowledge. Figure~\ref{fig:controlnet} illustrates this spatial conditioning pathway.

\subsubsection{Full System Overview: Training and Inference}\label{sec:overview}

Having introduced each building block in isolation (VAE-based latent compression, DDPM noise corruption, dual CLIP text encoding, and ControlNet spatial conditioning), we now describe how they compose into a single, end-to-end trainable system. The complete training data flow is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}.

    \centering
    \resizebox{\linewidth}{!}{%
        \sffamily
        \input{architecture.tex}%
    }
    \caption{Training architecture of Mask2Derm. \textbf{1.}~Input image $\to$ \textbf{2.}~frozen VAE encoder $\to$ latent $\mathbf{z}_0$. \textbf{3.}~DDPM noise scheduler adds $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ at timestep $t$ to yield $\mathbf{z}_t$. \textbf{4.}~Text prompt $\to$ \textbf{5.}~CLIP Enc.\,1 and \textbf{6.}~CLIP Enc.\,2 (frozen) $\to$ joint embedding $\mathbf{c}_t$. \textbf{7.}~Binary mask $\to$ \textbf{8.}~ControlNet (sole trainable module) $\to$ zero-conv residuals injected into \textbf{9.}~frozen SDXL U-Net. \textbf{10.}~Min-SNR weighted loss; gradients flow only through~\textbf{8.}.}
    \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Training loop.}
Each iteration samples a triplet $(\mathbf{x},\,\mathbf{c}_s,\,\mathbf{p})$ from the dataset. The four building blocks described above execute in parallel: the frozen VAE encodes $\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathbf{z}_0$; the DDPM scheduler adds noise at a randomly sampled $t$ to yield $\mathbf{z}_t$; the two frozen CLIP encoders produce $\mathbf{c}_t$; and the ControlNet processes $\mathbf{c}_s$ to produce multi-scale residuals $\{\Delta\mathbf{y}_l\}$. The frozen U-Net then receives $\mathbf{z}_t$, attends to $\mathbf{c}_t$ via cross-attention, incorporates $\{\Delta\mathbf{y}_l\}$ through its skip connections, and predicts the added noise:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}} = \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta\!\left(\mathbf{z}_t,\, t,\, \mathbf{c}_t,\, \mathbf{c}_s\right).
\end{equation}
The loss is Min-SNR-$\gamma$–weighted MSE~\cite{hang2023efficient} with $\gamma=5$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\min(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\,5)}{\mathrm{SNR}(t)}\,\left\|\boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \hat{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}\right\|^2_2\right],
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{SNR}(t)=\bar{\alpha}_t/(1-\bar{\alpha}_t)$. This caps the gradient contribution of high-SNR steps, balancing learning signal across the full noise spectrum. Gradients flow exclusively through the ControlNet; the U-Net, VAE, and CLIP encoders receive no updates. The optimizer is AdamW ($\mathrm{lr}=10^{-5}$, cosine decay, 1{,}000 warm-up steps); training runs for 150 epochs in BF16 with effective batch size 160.

\paragraph{Inference pipeline.}
At inference time the pipeline inverts the training process and requires no real image as input: only a binary mask and a text prompt. The procedure unfolds in four stages.

First, a latent vector $\mathbf{z}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ is sampled from pure Gaussian noise; this is the starting point from which the image will be generated. Second, the text prompt is encoded by the two frozen CLIP encoders exactly as during training, producing the joint embedding $\mathbf{c}_t$. Third, the binary mask $\mathbf{c}_s$ is passed through the now-trained ControlNet, which produces residuals $\{\Delta\mathbf{y}_l\}$ encoding the spatial layout of the lesion. Fourth, the frozen U-Net iteratively denoises the latent over $S=30$ steps using DPM-Solver++~\cite{lu2022dpmsolverpp}, a fast second-order ODE solver that delivers DDIM-quality results in roughly half the steps. At each step, Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)~\cite{ho2022classifierfree} is applied with guidance scale $w=9.0$ and ControlNet conditioning scale $0.8$, sharpening adherence to both the text and the mask:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}_\theta = \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t,\varnothing,\varnothing) + w\cdot\bigl(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t,\mathbf{c}_t,\mathbf{c}_s) - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t,\varnothing,\varnothing)\bigr).
\end{equation}
After all $S$ denoising steps, the resulting clean latent $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_0$ is decoded by the frozen VAE decoder $\mathcal{D}$ back into pixel space:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{x}} = \mathcal{D}(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_0) \in \mathbb{R}^{1024\times1024\times3}.
\end{equation}
The output $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ is a photorealistic dermoscopic image whose lesion is spatially bounded by the input mask $\mathbf{c}_s$ and whose visual appearance is conditioned on the text prompt $\mathbf{p}$. The entire inference pipeline (from mask and prompt to synthetic image) runs in under four seconds on a single A100 GPU.

\subsubsection{Mask Generation Pipeline}\label{sec:maskgen}

When real segmentation masks are not available, Mask2Derm includes an optional mask generation module: a lightweight DDPM~\cite{ho2020denoising} ($\approx$22M parameters) trained exclusively on binary dermoscopic lesion masks. The module is fully decoupled from the main synthesis pipeline; real masks, generated masks, and hand-drawn annotations are all interchangeable inputs to the ControlNet stage.

\paragraph{Architecture and training.}
The noise prediction backbone is a four-level encoder–decoder UNet with channel widths $[C,\,2C,\,4C,\,4C]$, skip connections, and self-attention at the two deepest levels. Timestep conditioning uses FiLM~\cite{perez2018film}: a sinusoidal embedding of $t$ produces a scale–shift pair $(\boldsymbol{\gamma},\boldsymbol{\beta})$ that modulates each Group-Normalised feature map as $\mathbf{h}'=\mathbf{h}\odot(1+\boldsymbol{\gamma})+\boldsymbol{\beta}$. The forward process follows the same DDPM marginal as the main pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:overview}), but uses a cosine noise schedule~\cite{nichol2021improved} with $s=0.008$ to preserve sharper binary boundaries at low noise levels. The training objective is a plain MSE loss (no Min-SNR weighting):
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_\text{mask} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_0,\boldsymbol{\epsilon},t}\!\left[\left\|\boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\phi(\mathbf{x}_t,t)\right\|^2_2\right].
\end{equation}
Training runs for 50 epochs with AdamW ($\mathrm{lr}=2\times10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, cosine annealing, gradient clip $1.0$).

\paragraph{Inference and post-processing.}
Sampling uses DDIM with $S=50$ steps ($20\times$ cheaper than full DDPM). The continuous output is binarized at zero and refined by: \textit{(i)}~largest connected component, \textit{(ii)}~morphological opening (disk radius 3), \textit{(iii)}~hole-filling, \textit{(iv)}~Gaussian smoothing ($\sigma=2$) and re-thresholding at $0.4$. Masks whose lesion area falls outside $[2\%,\,80\%]$ of the image, or where any foreground pixel lies within 4 pixels of the image border, are rejected. Accepted masks are upsampled from $128\times128$ to $512\times512$ via LANCZOS resampling.

\paragraph{What is learned and what is not.}
It is worth emphasising the deliberate asymmetry of the training regime. The full Mask2Derm stack comprises approximately 4.7 billion parameters: the SDXL U-Net backbone (2.57B), the ControlNet (1.25B), the VAE (84M), and the two CLIP text encoders (123M and 695M). Of these, only the ControlNet's 1.25 billion parameters are ever updated; the remaining 3.47 billion parameters are entirely frozen. This design choice is not merely computational convenience. Fine-tuning the frozen backbone on a medical dataset of $\approx$10{,}000 images would cause catastrophic forgetting~\cite{mccloskey1989catastrophic}: the pretrained representations of skin texture, lighting, and photographic structure built from billions of natural images would be rapidly overwritten by the narrow statistics of the dermoscopy corpus, collapsing output diversity and degrading visual quality. By confining all learning to the ControlNet, a module whose sole purpose is spatial layout control, the system retains the full generative capability of RealVisXL while acquiring the ability to localise lesions according to clinician-provided masks.
