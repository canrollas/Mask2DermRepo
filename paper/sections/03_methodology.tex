\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Dataset.}
We use HAM10000~\cite{Tschandl2018HAM10000} as our training source. Among publicly available dermoscopy benchmarks, HAM10000 offers the largest collection of expert-annotated pixel-level segmentation masks, comprising 10,015 images acquired across multiple institutions and imaging devices. This scale and acquisition diversity make it well-suited for training a generative model that must capture a broad range of lesion morphologies and imaging conditions. The dataset is split into training (80\%), validation (10\%), and held-out test (10\%) sets.

\paragraph{Optics-inspired standardization.}
Raw images sourced from heterogeneous devices differ in field-of-view shape, brightness roll-off, and lens geometry. To harmonize these differences, we implement a physics-based simulation of dermoscope optics consisting of three stages applied in sequence.

First, we define the radial distance $d(x, y) = \sqrt{(x - c_x)^2 + (y - c_y)^2}$ from the image centre $(c_x, c_y)$. This distance map drives both the circular aperture mask $M(d)$ and the vignetting falloff $V(d)$:
\begin{equation}
    M(d) = \begin{cases}
    1, & d \leq R \\
    \text{smooth feather}, & R < d \leq R + \delta \\
    0, & d > R + \delta
    \end{cases}
    \quad \text{and} \quad
    V(d) = (1 - \alpha) + \alpha \left( 1 - (d/R)^2 \right)^\gamma
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the aperture radius, $\delta$ is the feathering margin, $\alpha$ controls vignetting strength, and $\gamma$ governs the falloff rate.

Second, we simulate barrel distortion by remapping pixel coordinates via a radial displacement model:
\begin{align}
    X &= \frac{x - c_x}{f_x}, \quad Y = \frac{y - c_y}{f_y}, \quad r^2 = X^2 + Y^2 \nonumber \\
    x_d &= f_x X(1 + k_1 r^2) + c_x, \quad y_d = f_y Y(1 + k_1 r^2) + c_y
\end{align}
where $k_1 < 0$ induces the barrel effect. The final standardized image combines all three components: $I'(x, y) = I(x_d, y_d) \cdot M(d) \cdot V(d)$.

Each processed image–mask pair is then used as a training sample, ensuring the model learns to generate synthetic images with consistent optical characteristics.

\paragraph{Contrast enhancement.}
Dermoscopic images acquired under varying illumination conditions often exhibit low local contrast, obscuring fine structural details such as pigment networks and vascular patterns. To address this, we apply Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE)~\cite{pizer1987adaptive} to the luminance channel of each image in the LAB colour space. Unlike global histogram equalization, CLAHE operates on local image tiles of size $8\times8$ pixels, independently redistributing intensity values within each tile. A clip limit $\lambda$ caps the amplification of any histogram bin before redistribution, preventing noise over-amplification in near-uniform regions:
\begin{equation}
    H'(v) = \min\!\left(H(v),\, \lambda \cdot \frac{N_{\text{tile}}}{L}\right)
\end{equation}
where $H(v)$ is the histogram count at intensity $v$, $N_{\text{tile}}$ is the number of pixels per tile, and $L$ is the number of intensity levels. The clipped excess is redistributed uniformly across all bins. The enhanced luminance channel is then recombined with the original colour channels, preserving hue and saturation while improving structural visibility. We use a clip limit of $\lambda=2.0$ and bilinear interpolation at tile boundaries to avoid blocking artefacts.


\paragraph{Data augmentation.}
To improve generalization and reduce overfitting given the limited size of the training corpus, we apply stochastic augmentation exclusively to the training split. Geometric transformations (random horizontal flip with $p=0.5$, random vertical flip with $p=0.5$, and random rotation up to $30^\circ$) are applied identically to both the image and its corresponding mask to preserve spatial alignment. Colour augmentation is then applied to the image only, since altering the mask photometrically is meaningless: brightness, contrast, saturation, and hue are perturbed with factors of $0.3$, $0.3$, $0.4$, and $0.08$ respectively. To ensure strict synchronization between geometric transforms on image–mask pairs, both are processed under the same random seed at each iteration.

\paragraph{Normalization and mask conditioning.}
After augmentation, images are converted to tensors and normalized to $[-1, 1]$ via
\begin{equation}
    \hat{I} = \frac{I - 0.5}{0.5}
\end{equation}
which centres the distribution around zero. Binary segmentation masks undergo a separate conditioning preparation: each mask is resized to $512\times512$ using nearest-neighbour interpolation to preserve sharp boundaries, binarized at a threshold of 127, and replicated across three channels to form an RGB tensor in $[0, 1]$. This three-channel representation is required by the ControlNet conditioning encoder, which expects an image-like spatial input.

\subsection{Mask Generation Pipeline}

When real segmentation masks are not available, Mask2Derm includes an optional mask generation module: a dedicated Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM)~\cite{ho2020denoising} trained exclusively on binary dermoscopic lesion masks. The module is fully decoupled from the main image synthesis pipeline — real masks, synthetically generated masks, and hand-drawn annotations are all valid inputs to the ControlNet stage.

\paragraph{UNet architecture.}
The noise prediction backbone is a four-level encoder–decoder UNet with skip connections. Channel widths follow $[C,\, 2C,\, 4C,\, 4C]$ where $C$ is a configurable base count, giving a spatial bottleneck of $8\times8$ at training resolution $128\times128$. Every encoder level applies two residual blocks followed by a strided $3\times3$ convolution that halves the spatial resolution; the symmetric decoder uses transposed convolutions for upsampling and concatenates the corresponding encoder features (skip connections) before each block pair. Self-attention is inserted at the two deepest levels, allowing the model to reason about global lesion shape rather than only local textures.

Each residual block conditions on the current timestep $t$ via Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM)~\cite{perez2018film}: a sinusoidal embedding of $t$ is projected through a small MLP to produce a scale–shift pair $(\boldsymbol{\gamma}, \boldsymbol{\beta})$, which modulates the Group-Normalized feature map as $\mathbf{h}' = \mathbf{h} \odot (1 + \boldsymbol{\gamma}) + \boldsymbol{\beta}$. This lets the network adapt its behaviour continuously across the $T=1000$ noise levels without separate per-timestep parameters.

\paragraph{Forward diffusion and cosine noise schedule.}
The DDPM forward process gradually corrupts a clean binary mask $\mathbf{x}_0 \in [-1,1]^{1\times H\times W}$ by adding Gaussian noise. Because the forward chain is Markovian with Gaussian transitions, the noisy sample at any timestep $t$ can be computed in closed form:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t}(1-\beta_s)$ is the cumulative noise retention factor. We use the cosine noise schedule~\cite{nichol2021improved},
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\alpha}_t = \frac{f(t)}{f(0)}, \quad f(t) = \cos^2\!\!\left(\frac{t/T + s}{1 + s} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2}\right), \quad s = 0.008
\end{equation}
instead of the original linear schedule, because the cosine decay keeps $\bar{\alpha}_t$ high at small $t$, preserving more signal in the low-noise regime and producing sharper binary boundaries.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The UNet $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\phi$ is trained to predict the noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ added to a clean mask, given the noisy observation $\mathbf{x}_t$ and the timestep $t$. The loss is the mean squared error between predicted and actual noise:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_\text{mask} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_0,\, \boldsymbol{\epsilon},\, t}\!\left[\left\| \boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\phi(\mathbf{x}_t, t) \right\|^2_2 \right]
\end{equation}
The model is trained for 50 epochs with AdamW ($\text{lr} = 2\times10^{-4}$, weight decay $= 10^{-4}$) and a cosine annealing schedule. Gradient norms are clipped at $1.0$.

\paragraph{DDIM inference and post-processing.}
At inference, standard DDPM would require $T=1000$ sequential network evaluations. We instead use DDIM~\cite{song2022denoisingdiffusionimplicitmodels}, a deterministic non-Markovian sampler that achieves equivalent quality in $S \ll T$ steps. At each DDIM step, the network estimates the clean mask $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0$ from the current noisy state and re-noises it to the preceding noise level:
\begin{align}
    \hat{\mathbf{x}}_0 &= \frac{\mathbf{x}_{\tau_i} - \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{\tau_i}}\;\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\phi(\mathbf{x}_{\tau_i}, \tau_i)}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_i}}}\,, \nonumber \\
    \mathbf{x}_{\tau_{i-1}} &= \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_{i-1}}}\;\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{\tau_{i-1}}}\;\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\phi(\mathbf{x}_{\tau_i}, \tau_i)
\end{align}
We use $S=50$ steps, reducing inference cost by $20\times$. The continuous output is binarized at zero and passed through a fixed post-processing pipeline: (1) the largest connected component is retained, (2) morphological opening with a disk of radius 3 removes edge noise, (3) binary hole-filling ensures the lesion is topologically solid, (4) Gaussian smoothing ($\sigma=2$) followed by re-thresholding at $0.4$ produces clean boundaries. Masks are finally checked for plausibility — lesion area must fall between $2\%$ and $80\%$ of the image, and no foreground pixel may touch the image border — and upsampled from $128\times128$ to $512\times512$ via LANCZOS resampling.

\subsection{ControlNet-based Image Synthesis}\label{sec:controlnet}

The image synthesis stage takes a binary lesion mask and a text prompt as inputs and produces a photorealistic dermoscopic image. It is built on Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL)~\cite{podell2023sdxl} with a ControlNet~\cite{zhang2023adding} module for pixel-aligned spatial conditioning. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the seven-component inference pipeline: a \textbf{①~binary mask} (real or DDPM-generated) and a \textbf{②~text prompt} feed into the conditioning stage; the prompt is processed by \textbf{③~dual CLIP encoders}; the mask is processed by the \textbf{④~ControlNet}, which injects residuals into the \textbf{⑤~frozen SDXL U-Net backbone}; the denoised latent is decoded by the \textbf{⑥~VAE decoder} to produce the \textbf{⑦~synthetic dermoscopy image}.

\paragraph{① Input image and ② VAE encoding.}
Following the LDM framework~\cite{Rombach2022LDM}, all diffusion operations are performed in the compressed latent space of a frozen Variational Autoencoder (VAE) rather than in pixel space. The VAE encoder $\mathcal{E}$ maps a training image $\mathbf{x}$ to a latent $\mathbf{z} = \mathcal{E}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot s_f$, where $s_f$ normalizes the latent distribution. For SDXL's VAE, the spatial compression is $8\times$ in each dimension — a $512\times512$ image becomes a $64\times64\times4$ tensor. At inference, the VAE decoder $\mathcal{D}$ converts the denoised latent back to pixel space. Working in this lower-dimensional space makes diffusion training substantially faster and lets the model focus on semantic structure rather than low-level pixel statistics.

\paragraph{③ Noise scheduler.}
During training, independent Gaussian noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})$ is added to the clean latent $\mathbf{z}_0$ at a uniformly sampled timestep $t$ using the DDPM scheduler, yielding the noisy latent $\mathbf{z}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{z}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$. At inference, this process runs in reverse: the U-Net denoises starting from pure Gaussian noise $\mathbf{z}_T\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})$.

\paragraph{④ Text prompt and ⑤–⑥ dual CLIP encoders.}
SDXL conditions on text through two CLIP encoders in parallel~\cite{podell2023sdxl}. The \textbf{⑤~first encoder} (\texttt{CLIPTextModel}) produces token features of shape $[B,77,768]$ from the penultimate transformer layer. The \textbf{⑥~second encoder} (\texttt{CLIPTextModelWithProjection}) produces features of shape $[B,77,1280]$ together with a pooled global embedding of shape $[B,1280]$. The two token sequences are concatenated along the feature axis to form $\mathbf{c}_t\in\mathbb{R}^{B\times77\times2048}$, which is passed to all U-Net cross-attention layers. The pooled embedding is concatenated with image-size metadata and projected into the timestep embedding, providing a coarse global context signal. We always report original size as $1024\times1024$ regardless of training resolution, keeping SDXL in its high-quality generation regime.

\paragraph{⑦ Binary mask and ⑧ ControlNet.}
A plain SDXL model has no mechanism to constrain the spatial layout of generated content. ControlNet~\cite{zhang2023adding} addresses this by creating a \emph{trainable copy} of the frozen U-Net encoder and bottleneck blocks (component~\textbf{⑧}). The \textbf{⑦~binary mask} $\mathbf{c}_s$ — a three-channel tensor normalized to $[0,1]$ — is first encoded by a small trainable convolutional stack and added to the ControlNet's input. The ControlNet processes the mask alongside the noisy latent $\mathbf{z}_t$ and returns residual feature maps that are injected into the skip connections of the frozen U-Net decoder via \emph{zero convolutions}: $1\times1$ convolutions initialized with weights and biases at exactly zero. Denoting the frozen U-Net block as $\mathcal{F}(\cdot;\Theta)$ and its trainable copy as $\mathcal{F}(\cdot;\Theta_c)$, the residual at each skip connection is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{Z}\!\left(\mathcal{F}\!\left(\mathbf{x} + \mathcal{Z}(\mathbf{c}_s;\Theta_{z_1});\,\Theta_c\right);\,\Theta_{z_2}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{Z}(\cdot;\Theta_{z_i})$ are zero-initialized $1\times1$ convolutions. At step zero $\Delta\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{0}$, leaving the frozen backbone entirely unchanged; as training proceeds, the zero convolutions acquire nonzero weights and mask conditioning is gradually learned. The ControlNet weights $\Theta_c$ are initialized from the frozen U-Net encoder (\texttt{from\_unet}) to avoid any task-specific prior mismatched to dermoscopy.

\paragraph{⑨ Frozen U-Net backbone.}
The frozen SDXL U-Net (\textbf{⑨}) receives three inputs simultaneously: the noisy latent $\mathbf{z}_t$, the joint text embedding $\mathbf{c}_t$, and the ControlNet residuals. It predicts the noise $\hat{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}} = \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t, \mathbf{c}_t, \mathbf{c}_s)$. Only the ControlNet parameters $\Theta_c$ receive gradient updates; the U-Net, VAE, and both CLIP encoders are entirely frozen.

\paragraph{⑩ Training loss with Min-SNR weighting.}
A standard DDPM loss weights all timesteps equally, causing high-noise steps (low SNR) to dominate the gradient and preventing the model from learning fine detail at low-noise steps. We apply Min-SNR-$\gamma$ weighting~\cite{hang2023efficient}, which scales each step's loss by a weight inversely proportional to its SNR:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z}_0,\boldsymbol{\epsilon},t,\mathbf{c}_t,\mathbf{c}_s}\!\left[w(t)\left\|\boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t, \mathbf{c}_t, \mathbf{c}_s)\right\|^2_2\right], \quad w(t) = \frac{\min(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\,\gamma)}{\mathrm{SNR}(t)}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{SNR}(t) = \bar{\alpha}_t / (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t)$ and $\gamma = 5$. High-SNR (low-noise) steps receive small weights; low-SNR (high-noise) steps receive weight $\approx 1$. This stabilizes convergence and visibly reduces the block artefacts that dominate early training when high-noise gradients are unweighted.

Training runs for 150 epochs in BF16 mixed precision with AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2018decoupled} ($\text{lr}=10^{-5}$, $\beta_1=0.9$, $\beta_2=0.999$, weight decay $=0.01$), a cosine schedule with 500 warm-up steps, per-device batch size 20, gradient accumulation over 8 steps (effective batch 160), and gradient clipping at $1.0$.

\subsection{Classifier-Free Guidance}\label{sec:cfg}

Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)~\cite{ho2022classifierfree} allows independent control over how strongly the generated image follows the text prompt versus the mask condition, without requiring a separate classifier network.

\paragraph{Conditional dropout.}
During training, both conditioning signals are occasionally discarded with probability $p_\text{drop}$: the text prompt is replaced by an empty string and the mask by a zero tensor. This forces the model to also learn an unconditional distribution $p(\mathbf{z})$, so the same network can produce both conditional and unconditional noise estimates at inference.

\paragraph{Guided inference.}
At each denoising step, two forward passes are computed: one with the full conditioning ($\mathbf{c}_t$, $\mathbf{c}_s$) and one with both conditions dropped ($\varnothing$, $\varnothing$). The guided noise estimate linearly extrapolates from the unconditional toward the conditional prediction:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}_\theta = \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t,\varnothing,\varnothing) + w\cdot\bigl(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t,\mathbf{c}_t,\mathbf{c}_s) - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t,\varnothing,\varnothing)\bigr)
\end{equation}
where $w \geq 1$ is the guidance scale. Higher $w$ produces images that adhere more strictly to the mask and prompt, at the cost of reduced diversity. We use $w = 9.0$ as our default. Denoising is performed with DPM-Solver++~\cite{lu2022dpmsolverpp} and the Karras noise schedule~\cite{karras2022elucidating}, which together deliver high-quality samples in 50 steps.
